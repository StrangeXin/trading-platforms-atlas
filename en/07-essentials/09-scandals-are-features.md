# FTX / MF Global / MyForexFunds Are Features, Not Bugs

**One-sentence thesis**: Financial-industry "scandals" aren't mistakes — they are **natural equilibrium outputs of the incentive structure**. Each scandal triggers new regulation, the new regulation **strengthens the oligopoly**, the oligopoly produces the next scandal, and the cycle continues. Once you understand this, you won't be "shocked" by the next FTX — you'll know it's coming, you just won't know who.

## 1. Narrative vs. Reality

| Narrative | Reality |
|---|---|
| FTX was SBF's personal fraud | FTX was the **inevitable output** of the "exchange + hedge fund + market maker" structure |
| MF Global was Corzine's aggressive failed bet | MF Global was the **inevitability of broker-dealer + prop** |
| Mt. Gox was Karpelès's incompetence | Mt. Gox was the **inevitability of "world's first big exchange + no regulation"** |
| Regulators will prevent the next one | Regulation is reactive, not proactive — it can only "assign blame after the fact" |
| "Industry reflection" after each scandal | Each scandal accelerates **oligopoly**; the next one is bigger |

## 2. The General Pattern of the "Scandal Assembly Line"

Historically, every major financial scandal follows these 5 stages:

```
Stage 1: structural flaw exists
    Some business model inherently has a conflict of interest
    (e.g., exchange + custodian merged)

Stage 2: early players wrap "compliance" around the flaw
    They know the flaw, but operate "legally," keeping capture risk low

Stage 3: growth attracts more aggressive players
    Seeing incumbents earn, newer entrants push more aggressive strategies;
    the conflict deepens

Stage 4: one aggressive player blows up
    Market headwinds + leverage + mixed roles → capital shortfall → bankruptcy

Stage 5: regulatory response + incumbent benefit
    "Tightened" regulation → compliance costs rise → small players exit →
    incumbents' market share expands → cycle returns to Stage 1
```

## 3. Historical Case Library

### Case 1: Mt. Gox (2014)

**Structural flaw**: From 2011-2014, Mt. Gox was the world's largest Bitcoin exchange (60%+ share) while also acting as custodian + clearinghouse, with no external audit.

**Collapse**:
- Security vulnerabilities existed from 2011 (noted by early competitors like Coinbase)
- February 2014: announced loss of 850,000 BTC (then $450M, today $50B+)
- CEO Mark Karpelès was arrested and the firm went bankrupt

**"Preventing the next one" regulatory response**:
- Japan passed 2017 amendments to the Payment Services Act → required crypto exchange registration
- BitFlyer / bitFlyer and other Japanese exchanges used the moment to consolidate domestically
- **But**: Coincheck was hacked for $530M in 2018 (new "big event")
- **But**: FTX still happened in 2022 — regulation didn't prevent it

### Case 2: MF Global (2011)

**Structural flaw**: MF Global was broker-dealer + prop investment bank. Jon Corzine (former Goldman CEO, former NJ governor) took over as CEO in 2010 and used customer funds to speculate on Eurozone sovereign debt.

**Collapse**:
- During the 2011 Euro crisis, MF Global bought $6.3B of Italian / Spanish sovereign debt
- Market deteriorated → margin call → **tapped segregated customer accounts**
- Declared bankruptcy October 31, **$1.6B in customer funds "missing"**

**Corzine's personal outcome**:
- $5M civil settlement (with government)
- Lifetime ban from the futures industry
- **No criminal charges**
- Launched his own hedge fund in 2015, still operating

**"Preventing the next one"**:
- CFTC improved "customer fund segregation" audit requirements
- Dodd-Frank's Volcker Rule took effect
- **But**: the structural flaw (broker-dealer + prop mixing) **was not changed**

### Case 3: Alpari UK (2015 Swiss franc)

**Structural flaw**: Alpari UK was a retail FX broker running a B-book with 1:500 leverage, with massive long EUR/CHF retail positioning.

**Collapse**:
- January 15, 2015: SNB abandoned the 1.20 floor
- EUR/CHF dropped 20% in 30 seconds
- Customer accounts instantly went negative → negative balances
- Alpari UK's negative balances **exceeded its capital**
- Filed for bankruptcy protection January 19

**Same event at FXCM**:
- Customer negative balances: $225M
- Leucadia emergency rescue: $300M
- Acquired by Jefferies in 2017 → brand continued, company substantively gone

**"Preventing the next one"**:
- ESMA introduced "negative balance protection" in 2018 (brokers can't pursue retail beyond deposit)
- **But**: high leverage (30:1) **still retained**
- **But**: brokers began writing stricter "extreme event" clauses into their terms
- The next similar event: **brokers just don't pursue**, but retail still loses everything

### Case 4: FTX (2022)

**Structural flaw**: FTX was a crypto exchange + Alameda Research (prop hedge fund) twin structure. SBF and Caroline Ellison were the respective CEOs.

**Collapse**:
- Alameda used FTX user assets for high-risk investments (Luna, Solana projects, 3AC loans)
- May 2022 Luna went to zero + June 2022 3AC bankrupted → Alameda became insolvent
- FTX appeared to keep running, but $8B in user assets had been used by Alameda
- November 2: CoinDesk reported Alameda balance-sheet problems
- CZ (Binance CEO) then announced FTT liquidation → bank run
- November 11: FTX filed Chapter 11
- SBF arrested, sentenced to 25 years in March 2024

**"Preventing the next one"**:
- US passed FIT21 in 2024 → classified commodity vs. security
- SEC + CFTC began more aggressive enforcement
- **But**: **3 years after FTX's collapse, exchange + custodian merging remains industry default**
- **But**: DEX is retail-accessible but illiquid
- Bybit hacked for $1.5B in February 2025 — **a new "big event" within 2 years of FTX**

### Case 5: MyForexFunds (2023)

**Structural flaw**: Prop firms operated on demo accounts, claiming successful traders got "real capital" but never hedging to external markets.

**Collapse**:
- 2020-2023: MFF rapidly became the world's second-largest prop firm
- August 2023: CFTC sued for $310M "fraud"
- MFF ceased operations → users unable to withdraw

**Aftermath**:
- May 2025 Special Master report revealed CFTC procedural issues → some charges dismissed
- MFF already bankrupt; the suit was effectively **political theater for the CFTC**
- **Other prop firms' structural flaws unchanged**
- In February-March 2024, MetaQuotes "purged" other props, using MFF momentum + "US compliance" as cover
- December 2025: FTMO acquired OANDA → **prop + broker integration** became the new trend

## 4. Incentive Structure Determines Predictive Pattern

### Why every time plays out this way

1. **Structure itself permits it**: you can't fully "regulate away" exchanges also being custodians — DEX UX is too painful
2. **Players game the "legal boundary"**: firms walking the legal edge maximize short-run profit
3. **Aggressive players attract capital**: capital chases high returns → flows into aggressive players → they get more aggressive
4. **Black swans are inevitable**: there will be at least one extreme market event every 3-5 years
5. **Aggressive player blows up**: structure + aggression + black swan = bankruptcy
6. **Regulation is reactive**: **always one step late**, and easily influenced by industry lobbying

### Current future-scandal candidates (possible next FTX, 2026-2028)

**Candidate 1: one of the top prop firms**
- Execution risk of post-integration FTMO + OANDA
- If the FTMO-OANDA integration stumbles → could trigger mass withdrawals
- 2025 prop firm industry valued at $12B, head-concentrated
- **Probability**: medium

**Candidate 2: a crypto CEX + derivatives**
- Binance has settled with DOJ but systemic risks remain
- OKX / Bybit / Kraken derivatives business + prop market-making desks are combined
- The next crypto bear market + high leverage resonance = a major loss somewhere
- **Probability**: high

**Candidate 3: AI trading signals / bot services**
- 2024-2026 saw many "AI trading bots" emerge
- LLM-based signal services, auto-copy bots
- Statistically, "black-box blow-ups" are inevitable
- **Probability**: medium (small scale each, but many of them)

**Candidate 4: DeFi cross-chain bridges**
- 2022-2023 already saw Ronin / Wormhole / Nomad bridge hacks
- 2025-2026: as cross-chain DeFi adoption rises, expect more
- **Probability**: near 100%, only question is scale

**Candidate 5: a data provider or infrastructure attack**
- Bybit's 2025 Safe Wallet front-end attack was a prelude
- DeFi / CEX-depended infra (Chainlink oracles, RPC nodes, wallet UIs) are all single points of failure
- **Probability**: medium

## 5. Why Regulation Systematically Fails

### Problem 1: information asymmetry

- Regulator staff = college graduates + a few years of government work
- Regulated subjects = industry engineers / traders with 20 years of experience
- **The regulator's "experts" lag "innovation" by 3-5 years, always**

### Problem 2: incentive misalignment

- Regulator salaries are fixed; successful cases don't increase pay
- Industry pays 10× more: $50K → $500K / year
- Rational choice: **don't be too aggressive**, preserve future employment

### Problem 3: ex post regulation, not ex ante

- Nearly all major regulatory rules come **after** a major event
- SEC 1934 (1929 crash), Glass-Steagall 1933 (Depression), Dodd-Frank 2010 (2008), MiFID II 2018 (debt crisis)
- **No preventive regulation**

### Problem 4: technology lag

- In 2014 (Mt. Gox), most regulators didn't understand Bitcoin
- In 2022 (FTX), most didn't understand DeFi / staking / yield farming
- By 2026's possible AI-trading scandal, most won't understand LLM limits

### Problem 5: political-agenda interference

- Election cycles shift regulatory focus
- Parties have ideological preferences on "strict vs. light"
- Even if the regulator knows a problem, **the political environment determines enforcement**

## 6. Breaking the Cycle: Personal-Level "Scandal Risk Management"

### Diversify counterparty risk

**Don't put all assets with one institution**:
- Retail FX: spread across 2-3 large brokers (different jurisdictions)
- Crypto: diversify across Coinbase / Kraken / Binance + some self-custody
- Traditional equities: Fidelity / Schwab / multiple brokerage accounts
- **Any institution > 30% of your total assets is over-concentration**

### Watch out for "high yield + high exposure" players

Historically, **soon-to-fall firms** share these traits:
- Explosive user growth (AUM doubles in 6 months)
- Aggressive marketing (stadium naming, celebrity endorsements, political donations)
- CEO's loud public persona (SBF, CZ, Corzine)
- **Dramatically higher yields** than competitors (unrealistically high)
- Opaque or frequently delayed financial disclosures
- Heavy executive poaching from competitors

### Run a regular "breach drill"

Ask yourself:
- If the X broker I use went bankrupt today, what would I lose?
- If the Y crypto exchange I use was hacked today, what would I lose?
- If either answer exceeds 25% of total assets, reduce immediately

### Old-school principles

- **Self-custody hardware wallet** (crypto)
- **Segregated accounts / trust accounts** (equities / FX)
- **Instinctive skepticism of anything "too good to be true"**
- **If your broker is in Seychelles / BVI, don't deposit over $5K**
- **Withdraw regularly — make the broker prove it can actually pay out**

## 7. Regulatory Change: Signal vs. Noise

### Signals (will actually change industry structure)

- **Large license-threshold increases** (MiFID II-level rewrites)
- **Mandatory custody separation** (the biggest variable for crypto)
- **Personal CEO criminal liability** (e.g., SBF's 25 years)
- **Multinational concerted action** (not single-jurisdiction)

### Noise (sounds tough, accomplishes little)

- Fines (even $10B is a cost for big firms)
- Warning letters / public rebukes
- "We are investigating" statements
- Media coverage of regulation (vs. actual enforcement)

## 8. When Does the Cycle Stop

**Historical regularity**: the financial scandal cycle **won't stop until the structure changes**. Structural change requires:

1. **A disaster-scale event** (far beyond FTX) — the next crypto event with $50B+ in user losses could be the trigger
2. **Political consensus** (bipartisan / cross-aisle) — extraordinarily rare
3. **Technology substitution** (e.g., DEX UX truly catching up to CEX) — possible long-term, impossible near-term
4. **Generational handover** (younger people running regulation + tech) — a 20-year horizon

Near-term (2026-2030): **the cycle continues.**

## 9. Practical: How to Benefit from the "Scandals Are Features" View

### As an individual

- **Avoid becoming a victim**: diversify + self-custody + skepticism
- **Recognize signals**: high growth + high exposure + opacity = red flag
- **Don't "trust X won't fail"**: there's no too big to fail, only too slow to react

### As a developer / founder

- **Compliance infrastructure** is a growing market (every new regulation creates new compliance services)
- **Risk-management** tools are must-haves for brokers / exchanges
- **Independent audit / Proof of Reserves** remain under-served markets
- **DEX UX improvement** is the long-run direction

### As a researcher

- **Tracking structural flaws** is more valuable than tracking specific companies
- Wherever conflict of interest exists, the next scandal is brewing
- **Pass rate + actual enforcement strength** of regulatory proposals are the key metrics

## 10. The Final Conclusion

**"Was FTX an exception?"** — No. FTX was the **inevitable output** of incentive structure + absence of regulation + fast-moving crypto.

**"Can the next FTX be avoided?"** — No. The structure hasn't changed.

**"Will regulation really protect me?"** — Only so far. Regulation's objective function isn't purely "protect you."

**"What should I do?"** — Recognize this as a **cyclical phenomenon**, **run your own risk management**, **don't rely on regulation**.

This isn't pessimism. It's **sober realism**.
