# Charles Schwab

**The heavyweight champion of US retail brokerage by size.** 2024 AUC (Assets Under Custody) of **~$9.9 trillion** — roughly **50x** Robinhood's AUC. Founded in 1971, Schwab pioneered the US "discount brokerage" model, and its 2020 acquisition of TD Ameritrade cemented it as an **absolute oligopolist**.

## Key Facts

- **Founded**: 1971, San Francisco
- **Founder**: Charles R. Schwab
- **Public**: NYSE: SCHW
- **Headquarters**: Westlake, Texas (relocated from San Francisco in 2019)
- **Employees**: ~35,000 (2024)
- **2024 AUC**: **$9.9 trillion** (largest US retail broker; Fidelity is #1 by total AUM at ~$11T)
- **2024 revenue**: $19.6B
- **2024 net income**: $5.9B
- **Client accounts**: ~36 million

## History and Founding

### 1971–1975: A telephone broker takes shape

- **Charles R. Schwab** (born 1937) founded "First Commander Corporation" (later renamed Charles Schwab & Co.) in California
- Before **the SEC allowed free commission pricing in 1971**, every brokerage charged fixed commissions (starting at $65 per trade)
- On **May 1, 1975 — "Mayday"** — the day the SEC officially deregulated commissions, Schwab immediately cut his to **$8**
- He **invented the "discount brokerage"** model: no research, no advice, just execution at a low commission

### 1975–1990: Expansion

- **1976**: First retail branch opens in San Francisco
- **1980**: First broker to offer phone ordering (previously everything was mail or in-person)
- **1984**: Acquires Bank of America's discount brokerage operation
- **1987**: Lists on NYSE
- **1989**: Expands Schwab's first brick-and-mortar chain (the "Schwab 500" branches)

### 1990–2000: The internet revolution

- **1996**: Launches eSchwab, the first at-scale **online trading** broker
- **1999**: At the peak of the internet broker boom, Schwab's market cap surpasses Merrill Lynch
- **2000**: Acquires U.S. Trust (private banking)
- **2001 dot-com crash**: Schwab loses 60% of market cap and lays off a third of the workforce

### 2000–2020: The great consolidation

- **2004**: Charles Schwab (the founder) returns as CEO to turn the company around
- **2010**: Acquires optionsXpress (retail options)
- **2017**: US equity volumes push toward **zero commissions** (Schwab leads, partly in response to the Robinhood model)
- **October 2019**: **Schwab announces permanent zero commissions** (stocks, ETFs, options)
- **2020**: **Acquires TD Ameritrade for $26B** (bundled with the Pershing integration)
- Post-close, Schwab becomes the **clear leader of the US retail brokerage oligopoly**

### 2020–2026: The post-integration era

- June 2020: TD Ameritrade acquisition closes
- 2023: thinkorswim (TD's options platform) is integrated and becomes Schwab's flagship options front-end
- Q1 2023: The Schwab Bank unit goes through a liquidity crisis (an SVB-style episode at smaller scale) — Schwab Bank faces $41B in deposit outflows
- 2024: Recovery continues alongside further integration
- 2024: Becomes the second-largest provider of US retirement accounts / 401(k) plans (behind Fidelity)

## Business Model: Not Dependent on PFOF

### What makes Schwab different

Unlike Robinhood or TD Ameritrade, Schwab's revenue is **not primarily PFOF**:

| Revenue source (2024) | Share |
|---|---|
| **Bank net interest income** | 55–60% |
| **Asset management fees** (Schwab funds + advisory services) | 20–25% |
| **Trading-related** (commissions + add-on services) | 10–15% |
| **PFOF + other** | **5–8%** |

**Key differences**:
- Schwab owns **its own bank** (Charles Schwab Bank, FDIC-insured)
- Clients' **uninvested cash** sweeps to Schwab Bank, which earns the spread
- This is a more stable and much larger revenue source than PFOF
- **Zero commissions** was not an "innovation" for Schwab — it was a **revenue shift** (from commissions to net interest income)

### What "zero commission" really means

- In 2019, Schwab cut to zero first → forced TD Ameritrade and E*Trade to follow → triggered a wave of consolidation M&A
- Schwab doesn't depend on PFOF; it leans on banking and asset management
- But Schwab **still does PFOF** (just at a small share of revenue)
- Citadel and Virtu are the primary market makers for Schwab's retail order flow

## Unique Strengths

### 1. Banking

- Schwab Bank is a **genuine bank** (FDIC-insured)
- Client cash sweeps automatically to Schwab Bank
- Clients can bank, invest, and borrow from one institution
- This creates a **structural moat** against competitors

### 2. Retirement / 401(k)

- Schwab Retirement Plan Services is the #2 US 401(k) recordkeeper
- 401(k) is a top-of-funnel channel → converts to rollover IRA at retirement
- Clients become **sticky for life**

### 3. RIAs (Registered Investment Advisors)

- Schwab is the largest **RIA custodian** in the US (the custodian of choice for independent advisors)
- Large numbers of independent Registered Investment Advisors (RIAs) park their client assets at Schwab
- Each advisor brings $50M–$500M in assets
- It's an **institutional-scale** retail business

## TD Ameritrade Integration (2020–2023)

### Integration details

- June 2020: deal closes ($26B stock consideration)
- 2020–2023: 11M TD clients migrated onto Schwab's platform
- thinkorswim is retained (TD's crown-jewel asset)
- Some TD clients disliked the UI changes → a small slice churned out
- Q4 2023: integration essentially complete

### Why it matters

- The US retail brokerage **oligopoly is now locked in**
- Schwab + Fidelity + Vanguard + IBKR own 80%+ of US retail
- Other mid-sized brokers (E*Trade, Robinhood, etc.) **face an extremely steep challenge** in breaking into the top tier

## Controversies / Challenges

### March 2023 "Schwab-crisis"

- During the Silicon Valley Bank (SVB) episode, Schwab Bank also faced liquidity concerns
- Schwab Bank saw $41B of deposits **flow out** (clients moved cash into high-yield money-market funds)
- The stock fell 27% over the week
- Schwab shored up the balance sheet with its own capital plus partner liquidity lines
- **It didn't fail**, but the episode exposed the **interest-rate risk** inherent to the banking-heavy business model

### Regulation

- SEC + FINRA + CFTC (for the futures business) + FDIC (for banking)
- Multi-regulator oversight is expensive but politically stable
- 2023: Schwab reached a **$186M settlement** with FINRA (over TD-era compliance issues)
- Business continues

### ESG and politics

- Founder Charles Schwab is a conservative political donor
- The company is often seen as leaning conservative in "red state" politics
- Younger users often prefer **more progressive** platforms (Fidelity, Vanguard)

## What's Next

- **Crypto integration** (rolling out 2023–2025): Schwab plans exclusive BTC and ETH ETF products in Q2 2025
