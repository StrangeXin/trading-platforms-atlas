# Prop Firm Regulatory Pressure and Industry Consolidation: 2023–2026

## The Industry Before the Storm (Pre-2023)

By 2022–2023, retail prop trading had grown from a niche to a global industry with hundreds of firms and millions of paying traders. Key characteristics of the pre-crisis landscape:

- **~600–700 prop firms** operating globally
- Minimal direct regulation (evaluation challenges structured as fee-for-service, not securities)
- MetaTrader (MT4/MT5) dominated platform infrastructure (~48% market share among prop firms)
- Most firms operated through grey-label arrangements, accessing MT5 infrastructure via a licensed broker without direct MetaQuotes relationships
- U.S. clients were theoretically excluded (CFTC jurisdiction) but many firms accepted them informally
- Business quality ranged from professionally managed operations (FTMO, TopStep) to outright fraudulent schemes

## Trigger Event 1: MyForexFunds Shutdown (August 2023)

### What Happened

**August 2023**: The CFTC and Ontario Securities Commission (OSC) simultaneously acted against **MyForexFunds (MFF)**, a Toronto-based prop firm that the CFTC alleged had committed a **$310 million fraud** against retail traders.

The CFTC's core allegation: MFF represented that traders were trading real live accounts against third-party liquidity providers. In reality, traders were trading **simulated accounts**, with MFF itself as the counterparty — meaning the firm profited when traders lost.

### Immediate Industry Impact

- MFF's assets were frozen overnight
- Approximately $310M of trader funds became inaccessible
- Hundreds of thousands of funded accounts suspended
- The shutdown demonstrated that prop firm operators could face federal regulatory action

### The Twist: The Case Collapsed

This is crucial context often missing from initial reporting:
- A U.S. court eventually **dismissed the CFTC's case**
- Evidence emerged that CFTC representatives had **deliberately mischaracterized a tax payment** to obtain the freeze order
- The court ordered the CFTC to pay **$3.1 million in attorney fees** — extraordinary sanctions against a federal regulator
- MFF CEO Murtuza Kazmi was vindicated in U.S. courts; Canadian proceedings wound down separately

### Regulatory Ambiguity Exposed

The MFF case highlighted a fundamental legal ambiguity: **what exactly is a prop trading firm?**

- Is the "challenge fee" a regulated investment product?
- Is a funded account a form of securities transaction?
- Is the firm-as-counterparty structure fraud, or disclosed risk?

The CFTC and other regulators had no clean answer in 2023. That ambiguity was itself the regulatory risk.

## Trigger Event 2: MetaQuotes Crackdown (February 2024)

### The Policy

MetaQuotes systematically forced its licensed broker partners to terminate grey-label arrangements with prop firms, particularly those serving U.S. clients.

**Key dates**:
- **February 2, 2024**: True Forex Funds (Hungary) loses MT4/MT5 license — industry's first visible casualty
- **February 6, 2024**: FX News Group publishes exclusive story on True Forex Funds shutdown
- **February 11, 2024**: Purple Trading terminates multiple prop firm clients
- **February 14, 2024**: Blackbull Markets terminates Funding Pips

MetaQuotes' stated concern (per industry sources): exposure to U.S. CFTC jurisdiction through prop firms accepting U.S. retail clients via MetaTrader infrastructure.

### Scale of Damage

From February 2024 through end of 2025:
- **80–100 prop firms** ceased operations globally
- This represents **~13–14%** of all global prop firm operators in a single year
- MetaTrader's prop-firm market share: **48% → 24%** in 9 months

### Notable Firm Failures

| Firm | Date | Notes |
|------|------|-------|
| True Forex Funds | Shutdown May 13, 2024 | ~$1.2M in unpaid payouts to ~300 traders |
| SurgeTrader | Shut down May 24, 2024 | Platform closure following MT5 loss |
| The Funded Trader | Paused March 28, 2024 | Admitted >$2M in denied payouts |
| Funding Pips | Halted February 14, 2024 | Terminated by Blackbull; later migrated to alternative platforms |

## The Consolidation Dynamics

### Winners: The Established Firms

The crackdown was **disproportionately survivable** for larger, well-capitalized firms:

**FTMO**:
- Had sufficient scale and direct relationships with MetaQuotes to maintain MT4/MT5 access
- Had the cash ($211M) to absorb operational disruptions
- Had already begun diversifying platforms (DxTrade added late 2023 pre-crackdown)
- Grew revenue 53% YoY in 2024 to $329M — partly because competitor firms closed

**TopStep**:
- Futures-focused; operates under CFTC's existing futures/FCM framework
- MetaQuotes crackdown affected forex prop firms, not futures
- Partnered with Plus500 (Israeli exchange/broker) in a strategic deal
- Continued serving U.S. market through CME futures without regulatory disruption

**The5ers**:
- Israel-based; relatively conservative model focused on long-duration funded accounts
- Survived the crackdown; re-entered U.S. market in 2025

**FundedNext**:
- Bangladesh-origin; aggressive growth strategy
- Obtained brokerage registration in Comoros to gain MetaTrader license pathway
- Applied for licenses in Mauritius and Dubai
- Opened cTrader access to U.S. traders in 2025

### Losers: Mid-Tier and Opaque Firms

Firms that failed shared common characteristics:
1. **Dependent on grey-label MetaTrader** with no plan B
2. **Undercapitalized**: Low cash reserves couldn't absorb the operational disruption of platform migration
3. **U.S. client exposure**: MetaQuotes specifically targeted firms serving U.S. clients
4. **Questionable business models**: Some firms were running payout structures they couldn't sustain

## Platform Diversification Post-Crackdown

The MetaQuotes crackdown created an unprecedented demand shock for alternative trading platforms:

### Winners Among Platforms

| Platform | Owner | Key Prop Firm Wins (2024) |
|----------|-------|--------------------------|
| cTrader | Spotware Systems | FundedNext US, FTMO expansion, 300+ brokers/props |
| DxTrade | Devexperts | FTMO (added late 2023), multiple prop firms |
| MatchTrader | Match-Trade Technologies | Funding Pips (migrated post-MT5 loss) |
| TradeLocker | TradeLocker Ltd. | Several mid-tier prop firms |
| Sirix | Leverate | Some prop firm adoption |

### Market Share Shift

Finance Magnates survey data (2024):
- **62%** of traders still prefer MT5 (shows the depth of existing MetaTrader user preference)
- **27%** chose cTrader (massive gain from near-zero prop-firm share pre-2024)
- **9%** prefer MT4

This means even with 48→24% MetaTrader prop-firm infrastructure share, user preference for MetaTrader among those who used it remained high. The implication: as more prop firms re-obtain MetaTrader access (via regulated broker intermediaries), user churn back to MT5 is likely.

## U.S. Market Dynamics

### The Complexity

The U.S. market represents the largest pool of potential prop firm traders globally, yet CFTC regulations make it uniquely difficult:

- CFTC's RFED (Retail Foreign Exchange Dealer) registration required for firms dealing off-exchange FX with U.S. retail clients
- Prop firms structured as challenge/evaluation services occupy legal gray area — are they "dealing" FX?
- MetaQuotes took the view that its licensed brokers facilitating U.S. client access via prop firms created CFTC exposure

### Futures as the U.S. Safe Harbor

U.S. futures markets are regulated by the CFTC under a different framework (NFA membership for FCMs/IBs) that explicitly permits prop trading:
- **CME Group** futures contracts are legal, exchange-regulated
- **TopStep**, **Apex Trader Funding**, **OneUp Trader** operate legally in the U.S. because they use CME futures
- No MetaTrader required; CME's own Globex platform or third-party platforms (Rithmic, TradingView) are used

This is why futures prop trading **grew dramatically** as forex prop trading contracted in the U.S.:
- Per Finance Magnates 2025: "Futures now overtaking forex as the most-searched asset class among prop traders"

### FTMO's U.S. Re-entry via OANDA

FTMO's December 2025 OANDA acquisition is specifically designed to solve the U.S. market problem:
- OANDA holds CFTC/NFA RFED registration
- Under OANDA's regulatory umbrella, FTMO can offer forex prop trading to U.S. clients
- FTMO + OANDA's CFTC status = MetaTrader access for U.S. clients
- FTMO becomes the only forex prop firm offering MT5 in the U.S.

## Regulatory Developments (2024–2026)

### CFTC Rule 4.7 Amendment (September 2024)

The CFTC finalized amendments to **Rule 4.7** in September 2024, updating portfolio requirements for Qualified Eligible Persons (QEPs) with a compliance date of **March 26, 2025**. While not directly targeting prop firms, this narrows the scope of who can trade without full regulatory oversight.

### Digital Asset Market Clarity (CLARITY) Act (July 2025)

Passed the U.S. House of Representatives in July 2025. If enacted:
- Expands CTA (Commodity Trading Advisor) and CPO (Commodity Pool Operator) definitions to include digital asset managers and advisers
- Many crypto-focused prop firms would need to register with the CFTC as CTAs or CPOs
- Creates compliance costs that smaller firms cannot bear

### UK FCA Review

UK's FCA began examining prop trading firms' regulatory status in 2024–2025. Key question: do funded challenge programs constitute "arranging" or "dealing in" investments under UK financial services law?

As of April 2026: no final FCA determination published. The regulatory ambiguity continues.

### EU ESMA Examination

ESMA has been monitoring prop firm activities, particularly those marketing to EU retail clients. Concerns:
- Challenge fees may constitute investment products subject to MiFID II
- Profit-sharing arrangements may require authorization as portfolio management
- Marketing materials potentially violating ESMA's guidance on CFD promotions

## Industry Outlook: 2026+

### Near-Term (2026–2027)

Expected regulatory developments across multiple jurisdictions:
1. **Mandatory licensing** in UK, EU, and potentially U.S. for firms offering funded accounts
2. **Stricter KYC/AML** requirements (triggered partly by MFF-style fraud concerns)
3. **Profit-split transparency disclosures** required before traders sign up
4. **Standardized news-trading blackout periods** as regulators harmonize
5. **Capital adequacy requirements**: Firms must demonstrate sufficient reserves to pay out funded traders

### Medium-Term (2027–2030)

The most likely outcome is a **two-tier industry**:

**Tier 1 (Regulated Prop Firms)**: Large, well-capitalized firms (FTMO, TopStep, FundedNext) that obtain appropriate regulatory authorizations and operate as quasi-brokers. These firms will survive regulatory change but face increased compliance costs.

**Tier 2 (Gray Market)**: Offshore-registered, unregulated firms continuing to operate in regulatory gray areas. These will face periodic enforcement actions and reputation risks.

The number of prop firms will likely stabilize at **300–400 globally** (down from the pre-crisis 600–700) as regulatory requirements raise the bar for entry and operation.

### Long-Term Trend

The prop firm model's core economic logic — matching capital with trader skill, monetizing the evaluation process — is sound and unlikely to be eliminated by regulation. Regulation will professionalize the industry, not eliminate it.

The analogy is retail FX brokers in the 2008–2015 period: NFA/CFTC regulations eliminated many marginal operators but the market survived with better-capitalized, more professional firms.
